📐 Mathematics — Angles
Understanding Angles
Types, measurement and properties of angles
🤖
Shape Bot says: A 2D shape is FLAT like a drawing. A 3D shape is SOLID like a box you can hold! Count the sides and corners to name any shape!
🦸
Angle Hero tip: Think of a clock! 3 o'clock = right angle (90°). Between 12 and 3 = acute. Between 3 and 6 = obtuse. A flat line = straight (180°)!
📌 Remember: Acute < 90° · Right angle = 90° · Obtuse 90°–180° · Straight = 180° · Reflex > 180°
½ Mathematics — Fractions
Fractions
Understanding, comparing and calculating with fractions
🍕
Pizza Fraction trick: Imagine cutting a pizza! The bottom number (denominator) = how many slices total. The top number (numerator) = how many slices you eat! So ¾ means 3 slices out of 4!
📌 The numerator is the top number. The denominator is the bottom number. E.g. ¾ means 3 out of 4 equal parts.
🪞 Mathematics — Symmetry
Lines of Symmetry
Identifying and understanding symmetry in shapes and patterns
🦋
Butterfly test: Fold a butterfly in half — both wings match perfectly! That's symmetry! A square has 4 lines of symmetry, a circle has infinite, and a scalene triangle has NONE!
📌 A shape has line symmetry if you can fold it exactly in half and both sides match perfectly. The fold line is called the line of symmetry.
📏 Mathematics — Lines
Types of Lines
Horizontal, vertical, parallel, perpendicular and diagonal lines
🚂
Train track trick: Railway lines are parallel — they never cross! A + sign shows perpendicular lines — they meet at a perfect right angle (90°)!
📌 Parallel lines never meet · Perpendicular lines meet at 90° · Horizontal = flat like the horizon · Vertical = straight up and down
📊 Mathematics — Tally Charts
Tally Charts and Data
Reading, recording and interpreting tally marks
🖊️
Tally counting tip: Draw 4 lines, then a diagonal strike across them = 5! This makes counting in fives super fast. Then multiply groups of 5 and add the leftovers!
📌 Tally marks are drawn in groups of 5. Four vertical lines are crossed by a diagonal line: 𝄓 = 5

🔤 English — Word Classes
Word Classes
Nouns, Verbs, Adjectives, Adverbs, Pronouns, Conjunctions & Prepositions

Word Class trick — use a sentence! "The dog [noun] ran [verb] quickly [adverb] past the big [adjective] park [noun] and [conjunction] it [pronoun] was happy!"

⏱ English — Tenses
Verb Tenses
Past, Present and Future tenses — simple, continuous and perfect forms
⏰
Time travel trick! 📸 Past = already happened ("walked") · 🎯 Present = happening now ("walk / am walking") · 🚀 Future = will happen ("will walk"). The "-ed" ending usually signals past tense!
📌 Past: I walked · Present: I walk / I am walking · Future: I will walk · Perfect: I have walked
🔄 English — Active & Passive Voice
Active and Passive Voice
Understanding and converting between active and passive sentences
🐕
Dog bite trick: In active voice, the dog is the star — "The dog bit the boy." Flip it to passive: the boy becomes the subject — "The boy was bitten by the dog." Look for "was/were + past participle + by"!
📌 Active: The subject DOES the action — "The dog bit the boy." · Passive: The subject RECEIVES the action — "The boy was bitten by the dog."
🧩 English — Subject, Verb & Object
Subject, Verb and Object
Identifying and using the three core parts of a sentence
🐱
"The cat chased the mouse" — 🐱 The cat [Subject] 🏃 chased [Verb] 🐭 the mouse [Object]. Ask yourself: WHO did it? WHAT did they do? WHO/WHAT got it?
📌 Subject = who/what the sentence is about · Verb = the action · Object = who/what receives the action. Example: The cat [S] chased [V] the mouse [O].
💬 English — Phrases
Phrases
Noun phrases, verb phrases, adjective phrases and prepositional phrases
💡
Phrase vs Clause: A phrase is a group of words WITHOUT a subject+verb pair. "The bright red apple" is a phrase — no verb! But "The apple shone" IS a clause — it has a subject AND a verb!
📌 A phrase is a group of words that work together but do NOT contain a subject and verb pair. E.g. "the bright red apple" is a noun phrase.
📎 English — Clauses
Clauses
Main clauses, subordinate clauses and relative clauses
🧩
Clause test: Can it stand alone? "She laughed" ✅ — yes! It's a main clause. "Because the joke was funny" ❌ — no! It's a subordinate clause. Watch for words like because, although, when, if, since, unless!
📌 A main clause makes sense on its own. A subordinate clause does not — it depends on the main clause. E.g. "She laughed [main] because the joke was funny [subordinate]."
✏️ English — Sentence Formation
Sentence Formation
Building correct, varied and interesting sentences
✏️
Sentence builder rule: Every sentence MUST have a subject (who/what) AND a verb (action). Always start with a Capital letter and end with the right punctuation: . ? !
📌 Every sentence needs a subject and a verb. Types: Statement (.) · Question (?) · Exclamation (!) · Command (verb first)
